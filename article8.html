Attacks on the rise in Australia with critical national infrastructure in the cross hairs

By Thomas Hughes

28/02/2024





A surge in cyberattacks have been occurring in Australia, specifically targeting critical infrastructure, households and businesses, a recent government report has announced. This rise in attacks on Australia has been attributed partially to a new defence agreement between Britain and the U.S., which experts believe has drawn more attention from malicious cyber groups that are state sponsored.

Reports from the Australian Cyber Security Centre state that a rise of 23% in incidents relating to cybercrime equating to 94,000 counts have been reported during the financial year ending in June. This meant that an attack on assets owned by Australians occurred every six minutes. Richard Marles the Defence Minister highlighted the growing cyber threat in relation to an increased interest of attack stemming from state attackers in the targeting of critical infrastructure within Australia.

The AUKUS defence partnership which has focused on advanced military capabilities including nuclear submarines, has contributed to this growing threat landscape. Additionally, concerns have been raised regarding a state-sponsored hacking group which is located in China, This group which was identified by the Five Eyes intelligence alliance and Microsoft, poses a significant risk to CNI sectors such as telecommunications, energy, and transportation.

The average cost of cybercrime to victims has risen by 14%, this points to the need for collaboration between industry and government in cybersecurity efforts to lower this number. Not only this but a survey by the Australian Securities and Investments Commission revealed shocking gaps in cyber risk management among companies, this revealed inadequate measures to protect confidential data and a lack of cyber incident response plans.

The average cost of cybercrime to victims has risen by 14%, underscoring the urgent need for closer collaboration between industry and government in cybersecurity efforts. However, a survey by the Australian Securities and Investments Commission revealed concerning gaps in cyber risk management among companies, including inadequate measures to protect confidential data and a lack of cyber incident response plans.

Nigel Phair, a cybersecurity expert at Monash University, warns that cyber attacks against Australia will persist until organizations prioritize security and robust risk management practices. Recent incidents, such as the cyber event at DP World Australia resulting in a three-day operational suspension, underscore the urgency of strengthening the country's cybersecurity framework.

The imperative for such measures was underscored by the 2022 data breach at Optus, where personal information of 10 million Australians was compromised, prompting a review of the country's cybersecurity regulations.

Recommendations for Mitigation

Zero Trust Architecture: Adopting a zero trust security model, where no user or device is inherently trusted, can enhance overall cybersecurity posture. Technologies like identity and access management (IAM), multifactor authentication (MFA), and continuous authentication mechanisms can verify user identities and authorize access based on least privilege principles.

Advanced Threat Detection Systems: Deploying cutting-edge threat detection systems powered by artificial intelligence (AI) and machine learning (ML) can help identify and respond to cyber threats in real-time. These systems can analyze network traffic, user behavior, and system anomalies to detect potential attacks before they cause significant damage.

Endpoint Security Solutions: Implementing robust endpoint security solutions, such as next-generation antivirus (NGAV) software and endpoint detection and response (EDR) platforms, can help protect individual devices from malware, ransomware, and other malicious activities. These solutions should include features like behavioral analysis, sandboxing, and threat hunting capabilities.
